	On a northern, forgotten road out of Las Vegas is The Kali Tychi Casino and Hotel. Older souls remember the golden days of the casino. It was rumoured that the land it was built on was blessed to give the guests great fortune and luck. The casino faded into legend when the Nevada gambling ban hit in 1909.
	Over 20 years later, with the lifting of prohibition and relegalisation of gambling, casinos have been popping up in Vegas like wildfire. It was only inevitable that the famed Kali Tychi would capitalise on the resurgence.
	On the night of January 22nd, 1934, Aleks Apophis invites the public to join in celebrating the grand reopening. Following much anticipation, the doors begin to open. First to the wealthy elities, politicians, singers and movie stars. Next, to a select crowd of eager journalists. And finally, by 9pm, lucky members of the public are permitted entry.
	The evening's beginning is magical. Chips and drinks flow freely. A live jazz band echoes throughout the casino floor. In one of the larger forums, adjoining the primary poker room, a large black statue of a serpent's head is fixed above a pool of water. From its mouth, a river of mist seeps out, and onto the surrounding floor. Conversation is plenty and the experience is rich. As our session begins, the night is already in full swing.
